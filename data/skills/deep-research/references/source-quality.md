# Source Quality

Evaluate every important source before relying on it.

## Core checks

For each source, identify:

- Author or responsible organization.
- Publication date and update date.
- Source type: primary study, systematic review, dataset, official document, company disclosure, news article, commentary, or social post.
- Methodology or evidence basis.
- Geographic, temporal, demographic, or industry scope.
- Funding source or conflicts of interest.
- Whether the source cites primary evidence.

## Primary-source preference

Prefer primary sources when the research question depends on:

- Scientific findings.
- Legal or regulatory claims.
- Product specifications.
- Financial or market numbers.
- Policy outcomes.
- Technical capabilities.

Secondary sources are useful for context, but the final report should not treat them as stronger than the underlying evidence.

## Recency

For fast-moving topics, record the research date and prioritize recent sources. For historical or theoretical questions, older sources can be appropriate if they are foundational.

Always distinguish:

- Current evidence.
- Historical background.
- Outdated claims.
- Forecasts or speculation.

## Bias and incentives

Flag likely bias when a source:

- Sells the product or service being evaluated.
- Advocates for a policy under review.
- Has financial exposure to the conclusion.
- Uses selective benchmarks.
- Omits methods, denominators, or comparison groups.

Bias does not automatically invalidate a source, but it lowers standalone confidence.

## Inaccessible sources

If a source cannot be accessed:

- Do not cite it as if it was read.
- Mention that it was inaccessible if it materially affects the evidence base.
- Prefer an accessible primary source or a credible secondary source that quotes or summarizes it transparently.

## Source notes

Maintain short source notes during research:
