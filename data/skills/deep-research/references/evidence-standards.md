# Evidence Standards

Use these standards to decide how much confidence each conclusion deserves.

## Evidence hierarchy

Highest confidence:

- Systematic reviews and meta-analyses with transparent methods.
- Official statistics or primary datasets with clear methodology.
- Replicated randomized controlled trials when appropriate for the field.

High confidence:

- Large, well-designed longitudinal or cohort studies.
- Peer-reviewed primary research with clear methods and limitations.
- Official guidance, standards, or technical documentation from accountable institutions.

Medium confidence:

- Expert consensus statements.
- Cross-sectional or observational studies.
- Well-researched industry reports with transparent data sources.
- Company disclosures when checked against independent evidence.

Lower confidence:

- Expert opinion, editorials, interviews, conference talks, and blogs.
- Media reporting that does not link to primary evidence.
- Vendor claims, marketing material, or unsourced benchmark claims.

Very low confidence:

- Anonymous claims without corroboration.
- Single screenshots or social posts.
- Claims with missing dates, missing methodology, or unclear provenance.

## Multiple-source rule

Important conclusions should not rest on one source. If only one source exists:

- Mark the conclusion as low confidence.
- Explain why corroboration is unavailable.
- Avoid broad generalization.

## Contradiction protocol

When sources disagree:

1. State both claims fairly.
2. Compare source type, date, method, sample, incentives, and scope.
3. Check whether the apparent conflict is caused by different definitions.
4. Decide whether one claim is better supported.
5. If not resolvable, label the contradiction as unresolved.

## Confidence labels

Use these labels when summarizing conclusions:

- `[HIGH]`: multiple high-quality sources agree and methods are credible.
- `[MEDIUM]`: evidence is plausible but limited, mixed, or context-specific.
- `[LOW]`: weak, single-source, old, or indirect evidence.
- `[SPECULATIVE]`: hypothesis, early signal, or inference beyond available evidence.

## Red flags

Investigate or downgrade confidence when a source has:

- No publication date.
- No author or accountable institution.
- Unclear methodology.
- Conflicts of interest.
- Cherry-picked comparisons.
- Overgeneralized results.
- Unsupported numerical claims.
- Broken links or inaccessible primary evidence.
