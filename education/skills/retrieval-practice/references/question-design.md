# Question Design

Retrieval practice works best when learners reconstruct knowledge from memory. Design questions that require recall, explanation, comparison, application, or error correction.

## Retrieval types

Free recall:

- Learner receives no substantive cue.
- Strongest retrieval demand.
- Useful after learners have enough prior knowledge.
- Examples: "Explain...", "List and describe...", "Draw from memory...", "Write everything you remember about..."

Cued recall:

- Learner receives a partial cue, structure, scenario, diagram frame, or sentence stem.
- Useful for mixed-ability groups, newer material, language support, or misconception surfacing.
- Examples: "Complete the sequence...", "Use these three terms...", "In this scenario, explain why..."

Recognition:

- Learner selects from options.
- Weakest retrieval demand.
- Useful only as a warm-up, novice confidence builder, or diagnostic contrast.
- Should include distractors tied to real misconceptions.

Error correction:

- Learner diagnoses a flawed statement or worked example.
- Useful for surfacing misconceptions and improving transfer.
- The incorrect statement should be plausible, not silly.

Scenario prompts:

- Learner applies recalled knowledge to a concrete situation.
- Useful for causal reasoning, procedures, ethics, science, humanities, and workplace skills.

Diagram or model prompts:

- Learner reconstructs a visual, process, system, timeline, schema, or relationship map.
- Useful when spatial or structural understanding matters.

## Good retrieval questions

Good questions:

- Target meaningful concepts, relationships, processes, procedures, or misconceptions.
- Require the learner to produce knowledge, not recognize wording.
- Are answerable from memory if the learner understood the lesson.
- Make likely gaps visible.
- Avoid asking for obscure trivia unless the detail is central.

Weak questions:

- Give away the answer in the wording.
- Ask only "What is the definition of X?" repeatedly.
- Depend on exact wording from a textbook.
- Use multiple choice when free or cued recall would work.
- Reward guessing or familiarity instead of reconstruction.

## Recommended mix

For most mixed-ability classroom sets:

- 30-50% free recall.
- 30-50% cued recall, scenarios, diagrams, or error correction.
- 0-20% recognition.

For novices:

- Use more cued recall and one or two recognition warm-ups.
- Keep free recall shorter and more focused.

For advanced learners:

- Use more free recall, transfer scenarios, comparison prompts, and explanation prompts.

## Misconception targeting

If the user provides known misconceptions, include at least two prompts that surface them.

Use formats such as:

- "A student says... What is wrong with this?"
- "Which part of this explanation is accurate, and which part needs correction?"
- "Give an example that would disprove this claim."
- "Explain why these two ideas are not the same."
