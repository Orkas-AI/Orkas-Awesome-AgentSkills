# Thesis Workflow

Use this for topic selection, proposal, outline construction, literature review planning, and final self-check. Keep outputs diagnostic and structural; do not write the thesis for the user.

## Stage classifier

| User signal | Stage | First move |
|---|---|---|
| "I don't know what topic to choose" | Topic focus | Ask discipline, degree level, interests, available data, and constraints |
| "I have a topic but don't know how to start" | Proposal / outline | Convert the topic into research question, scope, method, and chapter logic |
| "My literature review is messy" | Literature review | Choose an organizing principle: theme, method, chronology, debate, or variable |
| "I wrote it but it feels weak" | Chapter diagnosis | Identify claim, evidence, explanation, and transition gaps |
| "I'm about to submit" | Self-check | Check format, citations, logic, method-result alignment, and common hard errors |

## Topic focus

Help the user narrow a topic by testing:

- scope: not too broad, not too trivial;
- researchability: sources, data, cases, or materials are available;
- contribution: clear gap, angle, or application;
- feasibility: method fits time, access, and skill level;
- supervisor fit: topic connects to department or supervisor expectations.

Good output:

- 3-5 candidate directions;
- difficulty / novelty / source availability notes;
- risks and narrowing suggestions;
- one question for the user to choose a direction.

Avoid:

- deciding the research direction for the user;
- promising novelty;
- generating a fashionable but infeasible topic;
- inventing data access.

## Proposal structure

Proposal guidance should frame these parts:

- research background and significance;
- literature status and gap;
- research question and objectives;
- research content or hypotheses;
- research method and technical route;
- expected contribution and limitations;
- schedule or milestones.

For each part, explain what it must accomplish. Do not write finished prose.

## Outline construction

Build a 2-4 level outline depending on discipline and degree level.

For each section, include:

- the role of the section;
- key claim or question;
- evidence or material needed;
- relationship to previous and next sections;
- estimated emphasis, not a mandatory word count unless the user provides school rules.

Check that:

- introduction leads to the research question;
- methods can answer the question;
- results or analysis connect back to the method;
- conclusion answers the question rather than adding new claims.

## Literature review organization

Choose one main organizing principle:

- theme or concept;
- method or model;
- chronology;
- school of thought / debate;
- variable relationship;
- application scenario.

A good literature review does not list papers one by one. It should show:

- what scholars agree on;
- where they disagree;
- what remains under-explained;
- how the user's project enters the gap.

## Final self-check

Use four lenses:

- Format: headings, numbering, figures/tables, formulas, page numbers, school template.
- Citation: in-text citations match the reference list; style is consistent; all borrowed ideas are attributed.
- Logic: chapters answer the research question; claims match evidence; no contradictions.
- Method: data, sample, tools, assumptions, and limitations are stated honestly.

Common hard errors:

- abstract does not match the paper;
- keywords are too vague;
- figures/tables have no titles or sources;
- conclusion overclaims beyond data;
- references appear in the list but not in the text, or vice versa.
